# Volume XXX Glossary

Definitions for terms introduced in **Volume XXX — Cisco CCDE Network
Design**, alphabetized. See also the [volume index](INDEX.md) for
pointers back to the chapter each term is drawn from, and the
[master glossary](../../GLOSSARY.md) for cross-volume terminology.

**CCDE (Cisco Certified Design Expert)** — Cisco's expert-level
network-design certification, comprising a qualifying Written exam
(400-007) and a scenario-based Practical exam, assessed on high-level
design and business alignment. Introduced in Chapter 01.

**Constraint** — A limit on the solution space — budget, existing
estate, team skills, timeline, regulation — that eliminates design
options; surfaced early because it narrows choices fastest. Introduced
in Chapter 01.

**Core module** — One of the first three modules of the CCDE Practical,
focused on Core Enterprise architecture and technologies. Introduced
in Chapter 09.

**Elective** — The selectable fourth module of the CCDE Practical,
determining the final scenario's technology area: AI Infrastructure,
Large Scale Networks, On-Prem and Cloud Services, or Workforce
Mobility. Introduced in Chapter 08.

**Failure domain** — The scope of the network affected by a single
failure; bounded by hierarchy and summarization, and sized to the
availability requirement. Introduced in Chapter 03.

**Gold-plating** — Adding capability no requirement asked for; a design
defect the CCDE penalizes as much as a missing requirement. Introduced
in Chapter 01.

**High-level design (HLD)** — The architecture level — topology,
protocol selection, addressing strategy, failure domains, service and
security posture — at which the CCDE operates. Introduced in Chapter
01.

**Implicit requirement** — A requirement not stated in a brief but
necessary to meet it (e.g., a merger implying overlapping address
space); where designs most often succeed or fail. Introduced in
Chapter 01.

**Low-level design (LLD)** — The implementation level — device models,
interface assignments, exact configuration and addresses — below the
CCDE's HLD focus. Introduced in Chapter 01.

**Operational sustainability** — Whether an organization can run a
design for years given its skills, tooling, and the design's
complexity; a first-class CCDE requirement. Introduced in Chapter 02.

**RPO / RTO** — Recovery Point Objective (tolerable data loss) and
Recovery Time Objective (tolerable recovery time); business-continuity
targets that drive redundancy and failover design. Introduced in
Chapter 02.

**SASE (Secure Access Service Edge)** — A design pattern converging
network and security enforcement at the cloud edge, so policy follows
the user without backhaul; central to workforce-mobility and
security design. Introduced in Chapter 07.

**Scenario method** — The disciplined approach to a CCDE Practical
module: read fully, extract requirements and constraints, answer the
question asked at HLD, justify from drivers, and revisit as the
scenario evolves. Introduced in Chapter 09.

**Trade-off** — The explicit choice between conflicting requirements or
constraints, naming the axis and the accepted cost; the unit of CCDE
design reasoning. Introduced in Chapter 01.

**Unified exam topics** — The single set of five weighted domains
shared by the CCDE Written and Practical exams in v3.1. Introduced in
Chapter 01.

**Zero trust** — A security-design model replacing location-based trust
with identity- and posture-based, least-privilege, continuously
verified access. Introduced in Chapter 07.
